Roana

漫行

Walk your world.

An open-source assistive navigation system for blind and low-vision users.

The Idea

Roana turns a smartphone — and later, a pair of smart glasses — into a quiet companion for going where you want to go. On-device computer vision watches what's ahead. The world arrives back to you as bone-conduction speech (what's around) and wrist haptics (which way to go). Your ears stay free for everything else.

The name comes from roam — the freedom and dignity of going wherever you want, on your own pace.

What Roana Is — and Isn't

It is

  • An electronic travel aid that augments the white cane and guide dog
  • Multi-modal: audio and haptic, working together
  • Built on off-the-shelf hardware you can buy today
  • Open source, AGPL-3.0

It is not

  • A medical device
  • A replacement for the cane, the dog, or O&M training
  • A vision-restoration product
  • For sale to anyone vulnerable as a paywalled service

How It Feels to Use

Audio
A quiet voice in your bone-conduction headset describes what matters: "step ahead, two meters," "crosswalk on your right." Your ear canal stays open. You still hear the world.
Haptic
A short buzz on your left or right wrist nudges you toward the safe path. Both wrists at once means stop. You learn it in an hour.
On demand
Press a button: "What's in front of me?" A larger model takes a closer look and tells you. Otherwise it stays quiet, watching.

Why Open Source

The blind and low-vision community has been burned too many times by accessibility tech that started open or free, then got acquired, locked down, and paywalled back. Roana is licensed AGPL-3.0, the strongest practical guarantee we can make that this won't happen here. Any modified version — including services built on top — must also be released under the same terms.

The code is on GitHub, free to study, free to fork, free to build with.
